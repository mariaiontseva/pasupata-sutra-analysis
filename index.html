Rope-bound (rajju-baddhaḥ) as (yathā) hawk (śyenaḥ) having-flown (gataḥ) even (api) is-pulled-back (ākṛṣyate) again (punaḥ), guṇa-bound (guṇa-baddhaḥ) likewise (tathā) jīva (jīvaḥ) by-prāṇa-apāna (prāṇa-apānena) is-pulled (kṛṣyate). A second analogy deepens the teaching: a hawk (śyena) rajju-baddha (tethered by rope) is pulled back (ākṛṣyate punaḥ) even after flying away (gataḥ api). Similarly, the jīva is guṇa-baddha (bound by the guṇas—material qualities/karmic tendencies) and kṛṣyate (is pulled) by prāṇa-apāna. The hawk appears free in flight but remains tethered; the jīva appears to act freely but is yanked back by breath and karma. The correlatives yathā...tathā structure the comparison. Liberation requires cutting both ropes: mastery of prāṇa-apāna through prāṇāyāma, and transcendence of guṇa-bondage through discriminative knowledge. In the same way that a hawk tied by a rope is pulled back when it moves, so the vital principle bound by the guṇas is pulled by prāṇa and apāna.
